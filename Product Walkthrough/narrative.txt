Act One — The world changed The core problem statement The security industry was built to detect. Nobody built the response. The threats are faster, cheaper, and more distributed than the industry that protects us. Drones over Langley. Kinetic strikes on hyperscaler data centers. 3,500+ physical breaches of the U.S. power grid in 2025. Active shooters in schools. Cartels at the border. Every one of these sites has cameras, sensors, alarms, guards, and five vendors. None of them have an operating system. None of them have accountability. The industry standard — “time to notify” — is a confession that the industry cannot stop anything. Three facts that frame the stakes (all open-source, defensible) 95%  of activity is missed after 20 minutes of video monitoring — human attention collapses (Sandia National Laboratories / DoE).  Live fact. 97%  of security alarms are false — 40%+ of U.S. cities have stopped guaranteeing police response (LAPD verified / Deep Sentinel).  Live fact. 100–300%  annual security guard turnover — the workforce protecting critical assets doesn’t learn the site before leaving it (BLS JOLTS / Security Industry Association).  Live fact. The reframe from “detect and notify” to “detect and defend” The industry’s best metric is  time to notify.  The new metric is  time to threat deterred, mitigated, or stopped.  That shift is the entire investment category. Deus X Defense is building for the new metric. Two analogies that make the category instantly legible Cyber for the physical world.  Twenty years ago, networks were defended by people with badges. Today, they’re defended by autonomous systems that detect, decide, and respond in milliseconds — with humans governing, not guarding. Physical security is where cyber was in 2005. The transition is inevitable. We’re building the operating layer for it. Fire safety codes, for threats.  Nobody asks whether a building should have a sprinkler system. Fire codes made suppression mandatory, ubiquitous, standardized, insurable. The same transition is coming for physical threats — autonomous detection, autonomous response, mandatory reporting, insurability. The question isn’t  if.  It’s  who writes the standard. (Use one or both, depending on room. Cyber analogy for tech investors. Fire code analogy for defense / policy / insurance audiences.) 4.
