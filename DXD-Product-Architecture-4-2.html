The Reality
The security industry sells pieces. Nobody owns the outcome.
A camera vendor sells cameras and moves on. A drone vendor sells drones and moves on. A sensor company, an access control company, a VMS platform, a guard company — each one delivers its product, hands the customer a manual, and disappears. Nobody integrates them. Nobody staffs them as one operation. Nobody takes accountability for whether the site is actually protected.
The people problem is just as structural. If the site staffs security in-house, they can't scale — trained operators who can coordinate drones, sensors, ground teams, and airspace don't come from the existing security workforce. So they outsource, and they get whoever the contractor sends that week — guards who may not meet the site's own standards, rotating through shifts with no continuity, accountable to the staffing company rather than the facility. The site has no single party responsible for the outcome. The camera vendor blames the integration. The integrator blames the staffing. The staffing company blames the tools. Nobody owns the security posture of the site.
The result: five vendors' hardware, five consoles, five support contracts, and a team that wasn't trained to operate any of it as one system. Cameras record but nobody correlates. Drones sit in docks because there's no pilot on shift. Sensors alert on everything. And after every incident, the same overstretched team that couldn't prevent it spends days reconstructing what happened from logs scattered across systems that never talked to each other.
The staffing problem compounds everything. If the site tries to run security internally, they can't hire fast enough, they can't retain specialized talent (drone pilots, AI operators, compliance specialists), and they can't cover every shift. If they outsource, they get a rotating cast of guards who don't know the site, don't have the training, and work for a company whose contract is measured in bodies, not outcomes. There is no single point of contact for whether the site is actually protected. The camera vendor blames the integrator. The integrator blames the staffing company. The staffing company blames the technology. Nobody owns the result.

The Foundation
CORTEX
Sense · Understand · Decide
A physical appliance that goes on-site, connects to every camera and sensor the facility has — any vendor, any age — and runs AI locally. DXD's operators see the site through CORTEX without touching the customer's network. The customer controls what's shared. And when the internet goes down, CORTEX keeps working.
The industry spent the last decade pushing everything to the cloud. CORTEX goes the other direction — deliberately. The same shift happening across AI right now, where localized processing is proving more capable and more scalable than routing everything through centralized infrastructure, applies directly to physical security. Video is high-bandwidth. Latency matters. Privacy matters. Uptime matters. An appliance that processes locally and connects to the cloud when it's useful — not when it's required — is the architecture that wins at critical infrastructure sites where connectivity is unreliable and data sensitivity is non-negotiable.

AI ModelsDetect + Learn + Discover
Tier 1 — Real-time detection. Object detection, classification, basic behavioral analysis. Runs on every frame. Frame-rate speed on the AI accelerator. For sites with Mitek edge panels, Tier 1 includes their licensed models (gunshot, weapons, behavior) at the endpoint.

Tier 2 — Cross-source correlation. Evaluates detections across multiple sources. Does this motion event correlate with badge activity? Does this vehicle match the pattern history? Is this behavior consistent with the site's normal activity? This is where confidence is built before alerting.

Tier 3 — Behavioral baselines. Learns what "normal" looks like at each specific site over time. Delivery schedules, shift changes, wildlife, weather-driven noise. Feeds back into Tier 2 — a known pattern gets suppressed with higher confidence, a deviation raises the score. Requires weeks of data before it's useful.

Tier 4 — Investigation and discovery. Retroactive analysis of buffered feeds (Mode 2) and proactive cross-event pattern matching (Mode 3). Computationally intensive, runs on-demand or as background batch.

Every operator correction — dismissing a false alert, flagging a miss — feeds the learning pipeline. Corrections from one site improve the models across every site in the network.

RecordSynchronized Incident Replay
The narrative, the timeline, the site map, and the evidence are one object. Selecting a sentence in the narrative jumps the timeline and map to that moment. Selecting a point on the timeline highlights the corresponding narrative and surfaces the evidence that informed the decision. A reviewer opens the secure COMMAND link and understands what happened without a phone call, without an email, without asking "what happened last night?" Every statement carries a source tag: CORTEX (system detection), SENTINEL (operator/system action), or AI (system recommendation). The reviewer knows exactly what the machine decided and what the human decided.
ReviewSecure Report Lifecycle
Assembly — When an incident resolves in SENTINEL, COMMAND assembles the report draft automatically. Timeline built from incident data. Narrative generated. Evidence linked. The draft is 80-90% complete.

Operator review — Refine the narrative, add context, correct details. The operator's judgment enters the record.

Secure review — The reviewer opens a secure link and sees the report, timeline, map, and evidence in one place. Role-based views: the operator sees edit controls and source tags. The reviewer sees comment and approve/reject. The authorizing official sees read-only with replay and download. A user pill in the topbar shows who's viewing.

Filing — Signs and locks the report. Digitally signed, timestamped, retained. The filed record cannot be edited.

Retention — Downloadable as PDF, accessible for re-review, retained under policy, and logged for audit. Every access, download, and review action is recorded.
