Act I · Helmand Province, Afghanistan

The weight of a decision made in a fog of war.

My platoon was pinned down in a high-stress ambush. I called in air support on a compound to save my men.

The next morning, The New York Times reported that we had killed fifty-two women and children. Whether that report was accurate or not — and the command investigation that followed disputed it — the burden was mine. The weight of that decision is mine still.

That is what the fog of war does. It makes a split-second call irreversible. It makes a young officer, trying to protect his men, a name in a headline he will never outrun. And it teaches a lesson that does not leave: the tools we give the people at the point of contact decide how the next tragedy gets written.

That is why I joined Axon. Not because I wanted to sell tasers. Because Rick Smith had a thesis I believed in: a world where violence and killing weren't necessary. Where the technology did the de-escalating, and the operator did the judgment.

Act II · Las Vegas, after Mandalay Bay

A naive question, and an answer that stopped me cold.

Shortly after joining Axon I met with the Las Vegas Metro Police Chief. We were in the aftermath of the Mandalay Bay shooting. 58 dead, 867 wounded.

We were walking through the response. The timeline. The heartbreak. I asked what I thought was a naive question: what if we had put a TASER on a drone and gotten it in the window in under a minute?

He didn't flinch. He had clearly thought about it. He said, plainly: "Over 50 lives would have been saved."

I didn't sleep that night. The answer was sitting right there — sixty seconds, a drone, a payload, an operator. The technology existed. The will did not. From that moment I became obsessed with how autonomy, robotics, and a faster OODA loop could be used to stop mass violence before it spread.

That obsession is not rational. It is not a market analysis. It is an oath I took when I was 23 years old, renewed every time another headline writes itself before the first responder reaches the door.

One thesis, stated out loud: remove the human from the point of threat.

In 2020 I took over TASER as SVP and GM. I ran the P&L, Robotics, New Ventures, and Hardware Engineering.

Act IV · Uvalde · the backlash · what I actually learned

We announced. The response was swift. Nine of twelve ethics board members resigned. I went to work understanding why.

By 2022 we had working prototypes. Then Uvalde happened. We watched it unfold live. Nineteen children. Two teachers. Seventy-seven minutes of police standing in a hallway.

We made the decision: we couldn't sit on the sidelines anymore. We announced the TASER drone publicly.

The response was swift. Reuters. NPR. The New York Times. Ethics boards. Editorials. Nine of Axon's twelve ethics board members resigned in protest. I did not hide. I went out and listened.

Reuters / NPR / NYT · June 2, 2022
Taser maker halts drone project; most of its ethics panel resigns

What I actually learned — four truths I owe the record:

  1. Private security needed to step up. The public sector couldn't — and shouldn't — carry the full load alone.
  2. Communities were rightly skeptical of a Big-Brother pitch from a taser company. Skepticism is not an enemy. It is a brief.
  3. Critical infrastructure operators wanted to control their own response. They couldn't wait on a call-for-service. They had targets on their back and obligations to shareholders and neighbors.
  4. Law enforcement is critically understaffed. Even where the will was there, the people were not. 8-12 minute average response. Seventy-seven minutes at Uvalde.

The answer wasn't just technology. The answer was responsible operators, coordinating with law enforcement, inside a unified system that could detect, respond, confirm, and stop a threat faster than a call for service. An integrated capability, not a product.

That is the lesson I carry into Deus X. Not the lesson of what the press said. The lesson of what the operators, the sheriffs, the plant managers, and the families told me when I shut up and listened.

Interlude · The Diagnosis

The physical world
is soft.

While the digital realm has been hardened by trillions in cyber spend, our physical infrastructure — power grids, water systems, data centers, stadiums — remains protected by a model unchanged in fifty years: unarmed guards, analog cameras, and hope. That model is collapsing under four forces, each accelerating.

Act V · Paris · the Olympics · the Innovator's Dilemma

It wasn't a product the market needed. It was an operating system.

For most of 2023 and 2024, I worked with the French Ministry of Interior on adopting robotic security for the Paris Olympics.

We heard the same thing, every meeting, every operator, every country: too hard. Regulation is complex. Training is a problem. Integration is a nightmare. Coordination with law enforcement is the biggest gap.

This was the Innovator's Dilemma, live. The market didn't need another point product. It did not need a better drone, or a better camera, or a better radar. Those exist.

The market needed a whole-product solution. An integrated platform. A single OS for physical security that absorbs every sensor, runs every workflow, coordinates with every responder, and reports to every regulator — in 90 seconds or less, from detection to confirmed-stop.

That is the realization Deus X is built on. Not a better widget. A sovereign operating system for the soft physical world.

Interlude · The Window

Why 2026,
not 2020.

Seven forces. In 2020 none of them were true. Between 2023 and 2026, all seven inverted. The window is twenty-four months wide. It has a close date.

Autonomy crossed the deployment line.
NVIDIA Cosmos, Isaac Lab, Omniverse, GR00T N1.6, Newton physics — an entire open stack for autonomous physical systems, delivered in 24 months. Boston Dynamics, Figure, Agility, Skild, Franka are all on it. The 1:N operator-to-system ratio that was science fiction in 2020 is standard operating doctrine in 2026. Deus X inherits the stack.
02
2024 — 2026

The targets got bigger, and the hyperscalers are paying.
Hyperscaler 2026 capex: roughly $700B. Five-year data center infrastructure spend: ~$7T. CHIPS Act: $500B+ in private commitments. Every dollar is a new physical target surface — substation, transformer, cooling array, fab clean room — that needs protection from day one. And the builders are now paying for it.
03
Aug 2025
JIATF-401
The Pentagon stood up a counter-drone command.
SecDef directed the establishment of Joint Interagency Task Force 401. Director reports directly to the Deputy Secretary of Defense. Authority to allocate $50M per initiative. The federal command structure for physical airspace defense — which did not exist in any form in 2020 — is being rebuilt around a single unified authority. Deus X already operates inside its orbit.
04
Dec 18, 2025
SAFER SKIES
State and local hands are finally unbound.
For the first time in U.S. history, state, local, tribal, and territorial law enforcement have a statutory path to detect · track · seize · disable · destroy drones threatening people, CI, or major events. $1B+ authorized through 2027. The authority that was federal-only in 2020 just became local.
05
Mar 16, 2026
FAA Part 108
BVLOS becomes the rule, not the exception.
The FAA's Part 108 final rule normalizes Beyond Visual Line of Sight operations. Routine flights, no per-flight permission, aircraft up to 1,320 lbs. Deus X holds the only nationwide 400ft BVLOS waiver on the books. When the waiver becomes an approval, we don't catch up. We were already there.

CIRCIA requires 300K+ CI operators across 16 sectors to report significant incidents in 72 hours, ransomware payments in 24. The rule is written for cyber, but the attack surface is physical too. Reporting requirements force detection requirements. Detection requirements force spend.

Act VI · Tallahassee · the second shooting · the decision

I watched Parkland survivors face a shooter for the second time in their lives. That was it.

April 2025. I watched the Florida State University shooting unfold — a campus where Parkland survivors had sought refuge, only to face a second active shooter in their lifetimes.

That was it. The integrators had it in their contracts. The OEMs had it in their roadmaps. The legacy security companies had it in their quarterly reports. None of them had it in their blood. It was going to take too long.

I stopped waiting. I left Axon. I assembled the best people I could find — operators, technologists, regulators, warriors. People who had been in the room when the decision had to be made. People who had carried the weight.

We built it ourselves.

We built it because the kids at FSU should never have had to do this twice. Because the 58 at Mandalay Bay deserved a sixty-second response and did not get one. Because the nineteen at Uvalde deserved an answer better than seventy-seven minutes in a hallway. Because the physical world is soft, and the people who are supposed to harden it have been waiting for permission, or for budget, or for the next committee, or for a better moment.

There is not going to be a better moment.
